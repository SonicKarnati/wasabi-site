## Strong Source-Grounded Brand Findings

- Wasabi should be framed around build-first AI education and capability creation.
- The source materials consistently support real outputs over certificates.
- Wasabi serves multiple audience tracks: students, parents, schools, SHG women, EFC leaders, government/public-sector stakeholders, institutional partners, startup builders, and young India builders.
- The strongest cross-document themes are human capability, confidence through creation, regional-language access, voice-first inclusion, practical outputs, and AI as a leverage layer.
- The strongest government/public-sector language centers on capability creation, welfare-to-capability transition, local-language building, proof of creation, and scalable public-interest models.

## Open Questions Before Public Website Copy

- What is the official primary domain and contact email for Wasabi?
- Which programs are currently active, completed, proposed, or only campaign concepts?
- Which impact metrics are verified internally and approved for public use?
- Which partner logos and third-party quotes have permission for website use?
- Can Adda247, Manus, OpenAI, UNESCO, NEP, India AI Mission, Haryana SCBC/MoSJE, and school/advisor references be named publicly?
- Are student photos, testimonials, project stories, and names cleared for public use?
- Which pricing, credits, software subscriptions, and program deliverables are current?
- What privacy, safeguarding, accessibility, and youth/student policies should be referenced?

## Claim-Safety Rules for Phase 2

- Treat achieved, projected, targeted, and aspirational claims separately.
- Use placeholders for all unverified metrics in wireframes.
- Do not show partner logos or quote third parties unless permission is confirmed.
- Do not present KASC projected metrics as achieved impact.
- Do not present proposed SHG/SCBC outcomes as measured results.
- Rewrite overclaims such as "Top 1% Global AI Skills," "smarter than most kids," and broad ranking claims unless verified and approved.
- Use "needs verification" notes wherever the source material makes strong social-proof claims.
