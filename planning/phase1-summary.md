## Key Brand Findings

- Wasabi AI is positioned as a movement and capability-building organization, not a generic course company.
- The central belief is that AI expands what human beings are capable of becoming.
- The brand should focus on people becoming builders through practical creation.
- "The Wasabi Moment" is the named breakthrough when someone realizes they can create.
- The strongest repeated language centers on building, capability, confidence, ownership, creation, real Bharat, and human potential.
- The brand should avoid overused corporate and EdTech language such as "empowerment," "upskilling," "future-ready," "cutting-edge," and "leverage."

## Key Visual Findings

- The approved palette is Deep Forest Dark `#11241F`, Wasabi Green `#2D6A4F`, Soft Terracotta `#E5784A`, Warm Cream `#FFF4E3`, and White `#FFFFFF`.
- Wasabi Green is the primary brand color. Terracotta is accent-only and should appear sparingly.
- Typography is defined as Cormorant Garamond for display/headlines and DM Sans for body/interface text.
- The visual signature is a hand-drawn single line used with real photos, outline/aura treatments, pure line illustration, or single concept illustrations.
- Photography should show real people, real classrooms, real workshops, real Bharat, and practical devices.
- The design system rejects gradients, decorative effects, sci-fi AI imagery, generic stock photography, crowded layouts, and unsupported fonts.

## Key Audience Findings

- Beneficiaries need to believe that AI is for them and that they can build something real.
- Government visitors likely need to see Wasabi as credible, practical, aligned with Viksit Bharat, and grounded in measurable human capability outcomes.
- International visitors likely need Bharat-native context explained without flattening the brand into generic global AI language.
- Institutional partners likely need operational clarity, proof, program structure, impact data, and credibility signals.
- Donors, sponsors, and volunteers are not explicitly defined as audiences in the current source materials.

## Major Missing Information

- Formal vision statement.
- Public contact details.
- Program names, structure, duration, curriculum, delivery model, and participant pathways.
- Impact metrics, reach numbers, and outcome evidence.
- Partner or institution proof.
- Awards, media mentions, certifications, or reports.
- Leadership, team, advisor, or board bios.
- Verified testimonials and case studies.
- Rights-cleared photography from actual Wasabi environments.
- Production-ready vector or transparent logo files.
- Accessibility, consent, privacy, safeguarding, or student/youth policy guidance.
- Government-specific and international-facing messaging details.
- Donor, sponsor, volunteer, or institutional partnership flows.

## Risks or Assumptions

- The current materials provide strong brand direction but limited external proof. A future website could feel polished but under-evidenced if credibility material is not gathered.
- The brand voice is distinctive, but public copy should be reviewed carefully to avoid drifting into unsupported claims.
- Government and institutional audiences are important in the Phase 1 brief, but the source materials do not yet provide enough proof points for those audiences.
- The available logo files are JPEGs only. Production design will need better logo assets.
- The brand calls for real human photography, but no rights-cleared program photography was provided in Phase 1.

## Recommended Next Step for Phase 2

Before website design begins, collect missing credibility and content inputs: program details, measured impact, partner proof, official contact information, team bios, testimonials, case studies, and production logo/photo assets. Then create a source-grounded website content map and information architecture for review before any page implementation.
