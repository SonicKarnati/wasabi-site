# Wasabi AI Site Strategy

## 1. Website Objective

The version 1 website should convince government, institutional, school, parent, and international visitors that Wasabi AI is a credible Bharat-native capability-building organization that helps people learn AI by building real things. The site should make Wasabi feel modern, trustworthy, practical, and socially meaningful, while avoiding unsupported claims. It should show clear program categories, explain who Wasabi serves, create confidence through source-grounded proof, and invite the right visitors to contact, partner, enroll, or review a program.

## 2. Primary Audiences

### Government and Public-Sector Visitors

- Who they are: Departments, welfare bodies, public education stakeholders, district/state officials, and policy-facing decision makers.
- What they need to believe: Wasabi can turn AI exposure into capability creation for students, SHG women, SCBC communities, and other underserved groups.
- Concerns they may have: Overclaiming, safety, accessibility, language, implementation capacity, measurement, and public-sector naming permissions.
- Proof they need: Pilot outcomes, verified program formats, local-language delivery model, participant outputs, privacy/safeguarding approach, and approved partner references.
- Action they should take: Start a conversation about a pilot, district program, school championship, or community empowerment initiative.

### Institutional Partners

- Who they are: Schools, colleges, training platforms, foundations, corporates, ecosystem partners, and event collaborators.
- What they need to believe: Wasabi can design and deliver structured AI programs with clear outcomes and strong learner engagement.
- Concerns they may have: Program quality, operations, brand fit, deliverables, credibility, and ongoing support.
- Proof they need: Program outlines, curriculum examples, deliverable lists, mentor/advisor information, student outputs, and verified partner/case-study proof.
- Action they should take: Discuss a partnership or program collaboration.

### Schools and Parents

- Who they are: School leaders, educators, and parents of Class 6-12 students.
- What they need to believe: Wasabi gives students safe, guided, beginner-friendly AI exposure through real projects.
- Concerns they may have: Child safety, difficulty level, coding requirement, screen use, academic relevance, pricing, and legitimacy.
- Proof they need: Class range, schedule, beginner FAQ, sample projects, mentor guidance, certificates, student stories with consent, and verified school metrics.
- Action they should take: Ask about AI Learner or school programs.

### Students and Young Builders

- Who they are: Class 6-12 students, college-bound students, startup builders, and young people interested in AI projects.
- What they need to believe: They can build with AI even if they are beginners.
- Concerns they may have: Difficulty, tools, whether coding is required, whether projects will be real, and whether they will get stuck.
- Proof they need: Clear projects, day-by-day flow, deliverables, community/mentor support, and examples from people like them.
- Action they should take: Explore programs, enroll, join a challenge, or contact Wasabi.

### SHG and Community Ecosystem Stakeholders

- Who they are: SHG women, EFC leaders, livelihood facilitators, community organizations, and institutions supporting women-led enterprise.
- What they need to believe: AI can be practical, voice-first, regional-language, and useful for daily business tasks.
- Concerns they may have: Literacy, device access, tool accuracy, privacy, sustainability, and measurable enterprise outcomes.
- Proof they need: Track structure, use cases, prompt handbooks, business deliverables, implementation gaps, and pilot data.
- Action they should take: Discuss implementing an SHG AI capability program.

### International Visitors

- Who they are: Global institutions, funders, academics, media, and potential partners curious about AI capability-building in Bharat.
- What they need to believe: Wasabi is not copying a generic Western AI education story; it is building a grounded Bharat-native model.
- Concerns they may have: Evidence, scale, governance, legitimacy, and clarity of context.
- Proof they need: Clear explanation of Bharat context, program models, verified outcomes, photos/stories with consent, and public-interest framing.
- Action they should take: Contact Wasabi for partnership, learning, or support.

## 3. Core Visitor Beliefs

1. Wasabi is legitimate and serious about human capability, not AI hype.
2. Wasabi has a clear purpose: helping people become builders in the age of AI.
3. Wasabi understands Bharat, including students, schools, SHG ecosystems, public-sector contexts, and young builders.
4. Wasabi can execute structured programs that produce real outputs.
5. Wasabi is worth contacting, partnering with, supporting, or enrolling in.

## 4. Site Map

### Home

- Why needed: First impression and credibility gateway.
- Question answered: What is Wasabi, why does it matter, and why should I trust it?
- Proof needed: Program categories, verified proof strip or placeholders, audience pathways, and contact CTA.
- CTA: Contact / Partner with Wasabi.

### About

- Why needed: Explains belief, philosophy, origin, and human-capability thesis.
- Question answered: What does Wasabi believe and why is it different?
- Proof needed: Brand belief, build-first philosophy, Bharat-native context, team/advisor placeholders.
- CTA: Learn about the work / Contact.

### Programs / Work

- Why needed: Shows operational clarity across different program types.
- Question answered: What does Wasabi actually do?
- Proof needed: Program cards for AI Learner, SHG Empowerment, public-sector POC, startup/hackathon programs, KASC/event programs, and co-branded bootcamps.
- CTA: Discuss a program.

### Impact

- Why needed: Gives trust, but must separate verified impact from planned scale.
- Question answered: What has Wasabi achieved, and what is still planned?
- Proof needed: Verified metrics when available, projected metrics clearly labeled, student/project stories with consent, case-study placeholders.
- CTA: Request impact details / Partner.

### Contact

- Why needed: Converts institutional, school, parent, and partner interest.
- Question answered: How do I reach Wasabi for the right kind of inquiry?
- Proof needed: Official domain/email/phone once confirmed, inquiry paths, program interest categories.
- CTA: Send inquiry.

## 5. Homepage Emotional Arc

1. Establish who Wasabi is: a Bharat-native AI capability-building organization.
2. Name the problem: AI access alone is not enough; the gap is capability.
3. Introduce Wasabi's role: people become capable by building real outputs.
4. Show the work: student, school, SHG, public-sector, startup, and event programs.
5. Build trust: proof strip with verified metrics or placeholders, program structures, and source-grounded credibility.
6. Show human proof: approved student/community story placeholders.
7. Invite action: partner, bring a program, enroll, or contact.

## 6. Trust Strategy

### Visual Trust

Use the core Wasabi identity from the design system: cream base, Wasabi green, forest dark, restrained terracotta, Cormorant Garamond, DM Sans, and generous spacing. Campaign visuals can appear as evidence within program cards, but should not override the main website identity.

### Content Trust

Use plain language, source-grounded claims, and clear labels for program status. Avoid inflated EdTech language. Separate "verified impact," "program design," "projected scale," and "needs verification."

### Institutional Trust

Show structured program formats, audience definitions, implementation requirements, and deliverables. Add partner/advisor logos only after permission is confirmed.

### Proof Points

Use placeholders until verified:

- `[Placeholder: verified students trained]`
- `[Placeholder: verified projects built]`
- `[Placeholder: verified schools/institutions reached]`
- `[Placeholder: verified partner proof]`
- `[Placeholder: approved student/community story]`

### Accessibility

Prioritize readable contrast, short sections, clear language, mobile-first structure, and accessible forms. For public-sector/community programs, include regional-language and voice-first delivery where supported by source materials.

### Avoiding Overclaiming

Do not publish third-party quotes, partner logos, projected metrics, student testimonials, ranking claims, awards, or public-sector naming until verified.

## 7. Version 1 Scope

### Include in Version 1

- Five-page website structure: Home, About, Programs / Work, Impact, Contact.
- Program category overview.
- Source-grounded brand language.
- Conservative proof placeholders.
- Clear CTAs for partnership/contact/enrollment interest.
- Review notes for missing content and verification.

### Wait Until Later

- Full program microsites.
- Final visual design system implementation.
- Online enrollment/payment flows.
- Partner logo wall.
- Detailed case studies.
- News/media library.
- Advisor/team profile pages.
- Animated storytelling.
- Production web app or CMS.
